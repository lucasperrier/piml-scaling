%%  ABSTRACT

\begin{abstract}
Physics-informed learning methods are typically evaluated at a small number of fixed model and dataset sizes, revealing whether a prior helps at a particular operating point but not whether it changes learning behavior systematically.
We propose empirical scaling laws as a diagnostic framework for physics priors and demonstrate the approach on fixed-horizon flow-map prediction across three dynamical systems: Lotka--Volterra (conservative, predator--prey), Van~der~Pol (non-conservative, limit cycle), and Duffing (conservative, cubic nonlinearity).
For each system, we compare a plain multilayer perceptron against physics-regularized variants---including ODE-residual priors at two discretization orders (midpoint and Simpson), and a structural prior (conservation or dissipation law)---fitting joint scaling surfaces of the form
\[
E(N,D) \approx E_\infty + aN^{-\alpha} + bD^{-\beta},
\]
where $E$ is test error, $N$ is model capacity, and $D$ is dataset size.

The experiments reveal a taxonomy of prior-induced scaling regimes that recurs across all three systems.
The single-step midpoint prior raises the error floor and flattens scaling exponents on every system tested (data-scaling slopes of $-0.06$ to $-0.01$ versus $-0.37$ to $-0.58$ for the plain baseline), with floor magnitude tracking the ground-truth discretization residual.
A higher-order Simpson prior partially recovers scaling everywhere but never surpasses the plain baseline.
Structural priors exhibit system-dependent behavior: the conservation-law prior nearly matches the plain baseline on conservative Duffing (slope $-0.44$ versus $-0.45$) but degrades to midpoint-level performance on non-conservative Van~der~Pol (slope $-0.06$).
On Lotka--Volterra, the conservation prior introduces bimodal optimization failures that are resolvable through lambda-ramp scheduling, identifying them as optimizer artifacts rather than intrinsic surface corruption.
These results establish that biased-residual priors universally raise error floors, that reduced-bias priors recover scaling in proportion to residual reduction, and that exact structural priors help only when the conserved quantity constrains the relevant degrees of freedom---three findings that hold across systems with qualitatively different dynamics.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Scientific machine learning (SciML) aims to combine data-driven models with scientific structure such as governing equations, invariants, symmetries, and conservation laws~\cite{willard2022,karpatne2022}.
A central hope is that such structure improves data efficiency, robustness, and interpretability.
However, comparisons between physics-informed and purely data-driven methods are often made at only a few fixed dataset sizes and model capacities.
As a result, it remains unclear whether physics-informed priors change learning in a systematic way or simply shift performance in isolated experimental settings.

In mainstream machine learning, empirical scaling laws have become a useful lens for understanding how test error changes as a function of model size, dataset size, and compute~\cite{kaplan2020,hestness2017}.
By contrast, comparable scaling-based evaluation is still underdeveloped in SciML.
This is a notable gap because scientific learning problems are especially sensitive to regime: task difficulty depends not only on sample count, but also on time horizon, parameter variability, discretization, optimization stability, and the form of the physical prior itself.

This paper studies a controlled instance of that broader question.
We consider fixed-horizon flow-map prediction on three dynamical systems---Lotka--Volterra, Van~der~Pol, and Duffing---and ask:

\begin{quote}
Do different physics priors produce distinguishable scaling signatures, and do those signatures recur across dynamical systems with qualitatively different structure?
\end{quote}

We study four classes of priors across a system ladder of increasing diversity.
The first is a single-step ODE-residual prior based on the midpoint rule; it is structurally meaningful but biased because exact trajectories do not satisfy the discretized residual exactly.
The second is a higher-order ODE-residual prior based on Simpson's $1/3$ rule, which reduces but does not eliminate the discretization bias.
The third is an exact structural prior---a conservation-law penalty on Lotka--Volterra and Duffing, and a dissipation-rate penalty on Van~der~Pol---that targets a known physical invariant or balance law.
The fourth is a composite two-step midpoint prior on Lotka--Volterra that completes a dose--response analysis on discretization order.
This design---biased and strong, reduced-bias and structured, exact but potentially weak, and a graded reduction series---enables a controlled comparison of prior--scaling interactions both within and across systems.

Our central claim is empirical and methodological.
We do not derive a general theory of physics-informed scaling from three ODE systems.
Instead, we show that the same qualitative scaling signatures recur across three dynamical systems with different conservation structure, nonlinearity type, and phase-space geometry.
Biased residual priors raise error floors everywhere; reduced-bias priors recover scaling in proportion to residual reduction; and exact structural priors help only when the conserved quantity constrains the relevant output degrees of freedom.
These regime types are \emph{not} visible in pointwise evaluations---they emerge only from the scaling surface.

The contributions of the paper are:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=0.3em]
    \item A controlled scaling-law protocol for evaluating physics priors on dynamical-system learning tasks, combining joint capacity--data sweeps, physics-loss diagnostics, and regularization-strength sweeps.
    \item A cross-system study spanning three dynamical systems (Lotka--Volterra, Van~der~Pol, Duffing) with four prior types, demonstrating that the same qualitative scaling taxonomy recurs despite differences in system structure.
    \item A three-way taxonomy of prior--scaling interactions---floor-raising (biased residual), scaling recovery (reduced-bias residual), and system-dependent structural---grounded in measurable ground-truth residual magnitudes.
    \item Evidence that exact structural priors are system-dependent: the conservation-law prior nearly matches the plain baseline on conservative Duffing but degrades to midpoint-level on non-conservative Van~der~Pol, with bimodal failures on Lotka--Volterra shown to be optimizer artifacts resolvable via lambda-ramp scheduling.
    \item A systematic ansatz comparison using five alternative scaling functional forms on the anchor system, confirming that the qualitative conclusions are robust to the choice of ansatz.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Physics-informed scientific machine learning}

Physics-informed learning methods inject scientific structure into machine learning models in several ways.
Soft approaches typically introduce residual penalties derived from governing equations~\cite{raissi2019pinn,cuomo2022}, with variants such as fractional PINNs~\cite{pang2019fpinns}.
Harder approaches build physical structure directly into the architecture or parameterization~\cite{willard2022,chen2018node}.
Operator-learning architectures such as DeepONet~\cite{lu2019deeponet} and Fourier Neural Operators~\cite{li2020fno} learn solution operators between function spaces.
These methods have been applied to fluid dynamics, climate modelling~\cite{kashinath2021}, and multiscale closure problems~\cite{sanderse2024}.
However, most evaluations are architecture- and problem-specific, comparing models at fixed dataset sizes and capacities~\cite{cuomo2022,ren2025}.
Physics-informed architectures also exhibit non-trivial optimization pathologies: PINNs can fail to train due to ill-conditioned neural tangent kernels~\cite{wang2022ntk}.

\subsection{Empirical scaling laws}

Empirical scaling laws describe how performance changes as a function of data, model size, or compute.
In natural language processing and vision, such laws have proven useful both descriptively and operationally~\cite{kaplan2020,hestness2017,henighan2020}.
They provide a compact summary of learning efficiency and can reveal regime changes or diminishing returns.
Recent work explains scaling phenomena through variance-limited and resolution-limited regimes~\cite{bahri2024}, while constructive models can predict generalization error across scales~\cite{rosenfeld2019}.
A recent survey documents similar power-law behavior across a wide range of machine learning settings~\cite{wang2024scaling}.

\subsection{Scaling as an evaluation lens for SciML}

The use of scaling laws in SciML is still limited.
Theoretical work on neural scaling for elliptic PDEs has begun to establish generalization bounds~\cite{lu2021elliptic}, but these results are confined to specific PDE classes.
There remains a need for controlled empirical work asking whether physical priors change the shape of learning curves in a reproducible and interpretable way.

\section{Problem Setting}

\subsection{Task formulation}

We study supervised fixed-horizon flow-map prediction for the Lotka--Volterra system.
Let $\bm{x}(t) = (u(t), v(t)) \in \mathbb{R}^2$ denote the system state.
Given an initial condition $\bm{x}_0$ and a fixed prediction horizon $T > 0$, the learning task is to approximate the flow map
\[
\Phi_T : \bm{x}_0 \mapsto \bm{x}(T).
\]
This formulation provides a well-defined supervised target while avoiding the confounding effects of long autoregressive rollout.

\subsection{Why fixed-horizon flow-map learning is a legitimate task --- and where its limits lie}
\label{sec:flowmap-legitimacy}

A natural concern is whether fixed-horizon flow-map prediction is itself an ill-posed or irrelevant learning task.
The literature suggests that it is neither.
Learning a flow map or evolution operator is an established paradigm in data-driven dynamical systems and operator learning.
In the dynamical-systems literature, flow map learning has been proposed explicitly as a framework for learning unknown dynamical systems from trajectory data, including partially observed settings where the governing equations are unavailable.
The central object is the time-$\Delta t$ evolution map taking a state at one time to the state at a later time~\cite{churchill2023fml}.
Related work extends the same perspective to stochastic systems by learning a stochastic flow-map operator rather than an explicit vector field~\cite{chen2024sfml}.

The task is also standard in neural-operator learning for PDEs.
Neural operators are designed to learn maps between function spaces, and a canonical time-dependent setting is to map an initial condition, coefficient field, or current solution state to the solution at a later time or next step~\cite{kovachki2023neuralops,li2021fno}.
For example, Fourier neural operator benchmarks for Burgers and Navier--Stokes are formulated as learning solution operators for time-dependent PDEs rather than identifying the governing equations directly~\cite{li2021fno,kovachki2023neuralops}.
From this perspective, fixed-horizon flow-map prediction is a legitimate surrogate-modeling task: it asks whether a model can approximate the evolution operator over a prescribed time interval.

However, legitimacy of the task does not imply that every physics prior is well matched to it.
Our negative midpoint result is better interpreted as a \emph{prior--task mismatch} than as evidence that flow-map learning is itself misguided.
The target in this paper is a coarse finite-horizon map $\Phi_T : \bm{x}_0 \mapsto \bm{x}(T)$, while the midpoint residual is a local-in-time numerical consistency condition imposed on the endpoints.
If that discretized residual is biased at the chosen horizon, then the prior can regularize the model toward an incorrect proxy objective even when the supervised task itself is perfectly meaningful.

Recent operator-learning work reinforces this distinction.
TI-DeepONet explicitly argues that conventional fixed-horizon rollout training can be limited because it predicts full spatiotemporal outputs while disregarding temporal causality, whereas purely autoregressive alternatives accumulate sequential error~\cite{nayak2025tideeponet}.
This does not invalidate fixed-horizon prediction as a task.
Rather, it shows that the suitability of the task depends on the scientific question being asked.
For surrogate modeling of a finite-time evolution operator, fixed-horizon learning is natural.
For evaluating whether a \emph{local} physics residual improves learning, the task may be poorly aligned unless the residual discretization is accurate at that horizon.

Accordingly, the main methodological lesson of this paper is not that flow-map learning is ill posed.
It is that one must distinguish between
(i) whether the supervised task is standard and scientifically meaningful, and
(ii) whether the chosen physics prior is compatible with that task.
The literature supports the former.
Our experiments interrogate the latter.

\subsection{Lotka--Volterra dynamics}

The Lotka--Volterra system is defined by
\begin{align}
\frac{du}{dt} &= \alpha u - \beta uv, \label{eq:lv-prey}\\
\frac{dv}{dt} &= \delta uv - \gamma v, \label{eq:lv-pred}
\end{align}
where $u$ and $v$ denote prey and predator populations, and $\alpha,\beta,\gamma,\delta > 0$ are system parameters.
We use fixed parameters $(\alpha,\beta,\delta,\gamma) = (1.5, 1.0, 1.0, 3.0)$ throughout.

The system possesses an exact first integral (conservation law):
\begin{equation}
H(u,v) = \delta u - \gamma \ln u + \beta v - \alpha \ln v,
\label{eq:lv-invariant}
\end{equation}
satisfying $H(\bm{x}(t)) = H(\bm{x}_0)$ for all $t$.
This invariant plays a central role in our experimental design: it provides an \emph{exact} physics prior that can be contrasted with an \emph{approximate} one.

\subsection{Van der Pol oscillator}
\label{sec:vdp-system}

The Van der Pol oscillator is a non-conservative system with a stable limit cycle:
\begin{align}
\frac{dx}{dt} &= y, \label{eq:vdp-x}\\
\frac{dy}{dt} &= \mu(1 - x^2)y - x, \label{eq:vdp-y}
\end{align}
where $\mu > 0$ controls the nonlinearity strength.
We use $\mu = 1.0$ throughout, which produces moderate nonlinearity and a limit cycle with period approximately $2\pi$.
Unlike Lotka--Volterra, this system has no conserved quantity: energy is pumped in for $|x| < 1$ and dissipated for $|x| > 1$, driving all trajectories toward the limit cycle.

The absence of a first integral is central to the experimental design.
On Lotka--Volterra, the conservation-law prior exploits an exact structural property; Van~der~Pol tests whether the same prior \emph{class} (structural constraint) works when the best available target is an approximate dissipation-rate balance rather than an exact invariant.
Specifically, we replace the conservation loss with a dissipation-rate penalty:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{diss}} = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{i=1}^{B}
\left(
\Delta E^{(i)} - T \cdot \mu\left(1 - x_{\mathrm{mid}}^{(i)\,2}\right) y_{\mathrm{mid}}^{(i)\,2}
\right)^2,
\label{eq:vdp-dissipation}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta E = E(\hat{\bm{x}}_T) - E(\bm{x}_0)$ is the energy change, $E = \tfrac{1}{2}(x^2 + y^2)$ is the harmonic energy, and the subscript ``mid'' denotes the midpoint $\tfrac{1}{2}(\bm{x}_0 + \hat{\bm{x}}_T)$.
This loss is structurally analogous to the LV conservation prior---it targets a global energy balance---but uses a midpoint approximation of the dissipation rate, making it inexact on ground-truth trajectories (GT residual $= 3.50$, comparable to the midpoint ODE residual).

\subsection{Duffing oscillator}
\label{sec:duffing-system}

The undamped, unforced Duffing oscillator is a conservative system with cubic nonlinearity:
\begin{align}
\frac{dx}{dt} &= y, \label{eq:duf-x} \\
\frac{dy}{dt} &= -\alpha_D x - \beta_D x^3, \label{eq:duf-y}
\end{align}
where $\alpha_D, \beta_D > 0$.
We use $(\alpha_D, \beta_D) = (1.0, 1.0)$ (hardening spring) throughout.
The system possesses an exact first integral:
\begin{equation}
E_D(x, y) = \tfrac{1}{2}y^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\alpha_D x^2 + \tfrac{1}{4}\beta_D x^4,
\label{eq:duffing-energy}
\end{equation}
satisfying $E_D(\bm{x}(t)) = E_D(\bm{x}_0)$ for all $t$.

Duffing serves as a stress test for the scaling taxonomy.
Like Lotka--Volterra, it is conservative, so the conservation-law prior is exact (GT residual $\approx 10^{-14}$).
However, its cubic nonlinearity produces stronger trajectory curvature, yielding larger midpoint residuals (GT residual $= 1.72$ versus $0.15$ for LV at $T = 1.0$).
If the midpoint floor tracks the ground-truth residual magnitude and the conservation prior recovers healthy scaling on this second conservative system, the evidence supports two claims: (1)~the floor-raising mechanism is universal and quantitatively predicted by the GT residual, and (2)~exact structural priors help when and only when the conserved quantity matches the system's geometry.

\subsection{System ladder: rationale}
\label{sec:system-ladder}

The three systems form a controlled progression:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
System & Conservation & Nonlinearity & Structural prior \\
\midrule
Lotka--Volterra & exact $H$ & predator--prey & conservation law (exact) \\
Van der Pol & none & polynomial $\times$ state & dissipation rate (approximate) \\
Duffing & exact $E_D$ & cubic & conservation law (exact) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Moving from LV to VdP removes conservation while keeping the ODE-residual priors (midpoint, Simpson) structurally identical---only the vector field $F$ changes.
This isolates the effect of the structural prior: if the midpoint floor recurs on VdP, it cannot be an artifact of LV's particular dynamics.
Moving from VdP to Duffing restores conservation but introduces stronger nonlinearity, testing whether the conservation prior's effectiveness depends on system geometry.
The central prediction is that if the scaling taxonomy is tied to \emph{prior class properties} (biased residual, reduced-bias residual, exact structural constraint) rather than to any particular system, the same qualitative signatures should recur across the ladder.

\subsection{Data generation}

For each system, initial conditions are sampled uniformly from a system-specific domain, and ground-truth flow maps are computed using the DOP853 solver (Dormand--Prince 8(5,3)) at tolerances $\texttt{rtol} = 10^{-9}$, $\texttt{atol} = 10^{-11}$, with prediction horizon $T = 1.0$.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
System & IC domain & Max solver error & Conservation / dissipation check \\
\midrule
Lotka--Volterra & $[0.5, 2.5]^2$ & $1.9 \times 10^{-9}$ & $|\Delta H/H| \approx 2 \times 10^{-9}$ \\
Van der Pol & $[-3, 3]^2$ & $3.8 \times 10^{-7}$ & N/A (non-conservative) \\
Duffing & $[-2, 2]^2$ & $2.5 \times 10^{-7}$ & $|\Delta E_D/E_D| \approx 7 \times 10^{-8}$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Each system's dataset comprises 20{,}000 training pairs, 2{,}000 validation, and 2{,}000 test pairs.
Training subsets of sizes $D \in \{64, 128, 256, 512, 1024, 2048, 4096, 8192\}$ are drawn as nested prefixes of the same shuffled training pool, ensuring that each smaller set is a subset of every larger one.
Three independent data seeds (11, 22, 33) provide distinct pools per system.

\section{Models}

\subsection{Plain MLP baseline}

Our baseline is a standard multilayer perceptron (MLP) with ReLU activations:
\[
\hat{\bm{x}}_T = f_\phi(\bm{x}_0),
\]
trained to minimize the mean squared error
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{data}} = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{i=1}^{B} \left\| f_\phi(\bm{x}_0^{(i)}) - \bm{x}_T^{(i)} \right\|_2^2.
\]
Input and output are standardized using training-set statistics; the loss is computed in normalized space.

\subsection{Capacity grid}

Model capacity $N$ (parameter count) is varied through a fixed architecture grid:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcr}
\toprule
Name & Hidden widths & Parameters ($N$) \\
\midrule
Tiny   & $[32, 32]$        & 1{,}218 \\
Small  & $[64, 64]$        & 4{,}482 \\
Medium & $[128, 128]$      & 17{,}154 \\
Large  & $[256, 256]$      & 67{,}074 \\
XLarge & $[256, 256, 256]$ & 132{,}866 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\subsection{ODE-residual prior (midpoint)}
\label{sec:midpoint-prior}

The first physics-regularized model augments the data loss with a midpoint-rule ODE residual:
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{total}} = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{data}} + \lambda_{\mathrm{phys}} \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{mid}},
\]
where
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{mid}} = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{i=1}^{B} \left\| \hat{\bm{x}}_T^{(i)} - \bm{x}_0^{(i)} - T \cdot F\!\left(\tfrac{\bm{x}_0^{(i)} + \hat{\bm{x}}_T^{(i)}}{2}\right) \right\|_2^2
\]
and $F$ is the Lotka--Volterra vector field from \cref{eq:lv-prey,eq:lv-pred}.
For Van~der~Pol and Duffing, the same formula applies with $F$ replaced by the respective system's vector field (\cref{eq:vdp-x,eq:vdp-y} or \cref{eq:duf-x,eq:duf-y}).
This is a first-order implicit-midpoint quadrature approximation to the integral form of the ODE, computed on denormalized (physical) state variables.

\subsection{Conservation-law prior}
\label{sec:conservation-prior}

The second physics-regularized model penalizes violation of the exact first integral:
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{total}} = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{data}} + \lambda_{\mathrm{phys}} \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cons}},
\]
where
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cons}} = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{i=1}^{B} \left( H(\hat{\bm{x}}_T^{(i)}) - H(\bm{x}_0^{(i)}) \right)^2
\]
and $H$ is the invariant from \cref{eq:lv-invariant}.
Unlike the midpoint residual, this prior has zero irreducible error: $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cons}} = 0$ on exact flow-map targets.
For Duffing, the same structure applies with $H$ replaced by $E_D$ from \cref{eq:duffing-energy}.
For Van~der~Pol, the conservation prior is replaced by the dissipation-rate prior (\cref{eq:vdp-dissipation}), which is structurally analogous but inexact.

\subsection{Composite two-step midpoint prior}
\label{sec:composite-prior}

The third physics-regularized model uses a composite midpoint rule with two half-steps, reducing the discretization bias of the single-step midpoint.
The model outputs a 4-dimensional vector $(\hat{\bm{x}}_{T/2}, \hat{\bm{x}}_T) \in \mathbb{R}^4$, predicting both the midpoint state and the final state.
Only $\hat{\bm{x}}_T$ contributes to the data loss; $\hat{\bm{x}}_{T/2}$ is shaped entirely by the physics loss:
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{total}} = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{data}}(\hat{\bm{x}}_T, \bm{x}_T) + \lambda_{\mathrm{phys}} \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{comp}},
\]
where the composite residual applies the midpoint rule on each half-interval:
\begin{align}
\bm{r}_a &= \hat{\bm{x}}_{T/2} - \bm{x}_0 - \tfrac{T}{2}\, F\!\left(\tfrac{\bm{x}_0 + \hat{\bm{x}}_{T/2}}{2}\right), \label{eq:comp-ra} \\
\bm{r}_b &= \hat{\bm{x}}_T - \hat{\bm{x}}_{T/2} - \tfrac{T}{2}\, F\!\left(\tfrac{\hat{\bm{x}}_{T/2} + \hat{\bm{x}}_T}{2}\right), \label{eq:comp-rb} \\
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{comp}} &= \frac{1}{B}\sum_{i=1}^{B} \left(\|\bm{r}_a^{(i)}\|^2 + \|\bm{r}_b^{(i)}\|^2\right). \notag
\end{align}
Because the midpoint rule has local truncation error $O(h^3)$ where $h$ is the step size, halving the step from $T$ to $T/2$ reduces the residual on ground-truth targets by approximately $2^2 = 4\times$ per step, and the two-step composite achieves $\sim\!24\times$ lower physics loss on ground truth compared to single-step midpoint ($\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{comp}}^* \approx 0.006$ vs.\ $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{mid}}^* \approx 0.151$).
This design tests whether reducing---but not eliminating---the discretization bias recovers healthy scaling behavior.

\subsection{Simpson's-rule prior}
\label{sec:simpson-prior}

To extend the bias-reduction series from second order (midpoint) to fourth order, we introduce a Simpson's $1/3$-rule prior.
As with the composite model, the network predicts an intermediate state, outputting $(\hat{\bm{x}}_{T/2}, \hat{\bm{x}}_T) \in \mathbb{R}^4$.
The Simpson residual applies the classical quadrature rule:
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{simp}} = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{i=1}^{B} \left\|
\hat{\bm{x}}_T^{(i)} - \bm{x}_0^{(i)} - \frac{T}{6}\left[
F(\bm{x}_0^{(i)}) + 4F(\hat{\bm{x}}_{T/2}^{(i)}) + F(\hat{\bm{x}}_T^{(i)})
\right]
\right\|_2^2.
\]
Simpson's rule is fourth-order accurate ($O(h^5)$ local truncation error), reducing the ground-truth residual by a further $\sim\!14\times$ relative to the composite midpoint on LV ($\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{simp}}^* \approx 4.4 \times 10^{-4}$ vs.\ $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{comp}}^* \approx 0.006$).
The Simpson prior is applied to all three systems with the same formula, substituting only the vector field $F$.

This creates a graded bias-reduction series---midpoint ($O(h^3)$), composite midpoint ($O(h^3)$ with $h \to h/2$), Simpson ($O(h^5)$)---enabling a dose--response analysis: if the error floor is causally linked to the ground-truth residual, lower-order bias should monotonically produce higher floors.

\section{Experimental Design}

\subsection{Scaling axes}

We vary two quantities systematically:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Model capacity} $N$: five sizes from 1{,}218 to 132{,}866 parameters.
    \item \textbf{Dataset size} $D$: eight sizes from 64 to 8{,}192 training samples.
\end{itemize}
The use of nested subsets reduces confounding due to sample composition.

\subsection{Evaluation metric}

The primary metric is the mean relative $\ell_2$ prediction error on the held-out test set, computed in physical (denormalized) coordinates:
\[
E(N,D) = \frac{1}{M}\sum_{i=1}^{M}
\frac{\left\| \hat{\bm{x}}_T^{(i)} - \bm{x}_T^{(i)} \right\|_2}
{\left\| \bm{x}_T^{(i)} \right\|_2 + \varepsilon},
\]
where $\varepsilon > 0$ is a small stability constant.

\subsection{Scaling-law ansatz}

We fit empirical relationships of the form
\begin{equation}
E(N,D) \approx E_\infty + aN^{-\alpha} + bD^{-\beta},
\label{eq:scaling}
\end{equation}
where $E_\infty$ is an asymptotic error floor, $\alpha$ is the capacity-scaling exponent, $\beta$ is the data-scaling exponent, and $a,b > 0$ are fitted coefficients.
The purpose is descriptive and comparative, not predictive.

\subsection{Statistical protocol}

For the anchor system (Lotka--Volterra), each $(N, D)$ configuration is run across 3 data seeds $\times$ 3 training seeds $= 9$ replicates.
For the cross-system pilot sweeps (Van~der~Pol and Duffing), each configuration uses 1 data seed $\times$ 2 training seeds $= 2$ replicates at three capacities (small, medium, large) and three dataset sizes ($D \in \{128, 512, 2048\}$), yielding 72 runs per system.
The pilot grid is sufficient to establish qualitative regime classification; full sweeps with bootstrap confidence intervals are deferred to future work.
Scaling-law parameters are estimated with 1{,}000 bootstrap samples on LV to produce 95\% confidence intervals.

\subsection{Training details}

All models use Adam with learning rate $10^{-3}$, weight decay $10^{-6}$, batch size $\min(128, D)$, and train for up to 400 epochs with early stopping (patience 40 on validation relative $\ell_2$).
The composite model has a 4-dimensional output; only the last two components ($\hat{\bm{x}}_T$) are used for the data loss and evaluation metric.
The full grid comprises $5 \times 8 \times 9 = 360$ runs per model on LV, totaling 1{,}080 production runs for the three LV scaling-law models (plain, midpoint, composite), plus 126 conservation-law $\lambda$-sweep runs, 324 midpoint $\lambda$-sweep runs, 810 conservation-rescue runs, and $2 \times 72 = 144$ cross-system pilot runs (Van~der~Pol and Duffing).

\section{Research Questions}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=0.3em]
    \item Do physics priors produce distinct, interpretable signatures in the scaling surface $E(N,D)$, and do those signatures recur across dynamical systems?
    \item Does reducing the discretization bias of an ODE-residual prior recover healthy scaling, and does the degree of recovery track the ground-truth residual magnitude?
    \item Do exact structural priors (conservation, dissipation) help or hurt, and does the answer depend on whether the system actually possesses the targeted invariant?
    \item Can scaling exponents and error floors diagnose prior quality more informatively than pointwise comparisons?
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
The midpoint-rule residual is a first-order quadrature approximation.
At the tested horizon $T = 1.0$, even ground-truth flow-map targets incur a mean relative residual of approximately 15\% under this approximation.
The physics loss therefore has an irreducible floor of $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{mid}}^* \approx 0.15$, which is $\sim\!50{,}000\times$ larger than the achievable data loss ($\sim\!3 \times 10^{-6}$).
As training progresses and data loss decreases, the physics gradient dominates, and the optimizer converges to a plateau determined by the biased prior rather than the data.

\subsection{Conservation-law prior: bimodal optimization failure}
\label{sec:results-conservation}

The conservation-law prior, despite having zero irreducible error on ground-truth targets ($\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cons}}^* \approx 10^{-14}$), exhibits a qualitatively different failure mode from the midpoint prior.
Rather than a deterministic error floor, the conservation prior introduces a \emph{bimodal optimization failure}: depending on the random seed, training either converges to the correct basin (matching the plain baseline) or traps in a bad local minimum at test error $\approx 0.09$.

A $\lambda$ sweep across 7 values ($\lambda \in \{0, 10^{-4}, 10^{-3}, 10^{-2}, 10^{-1}, 1, 10\}$) at two capacities and three dataset sizes (126 runs total) reveals the pattern:

\paragraph{Key observations.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item The large model ($N = 67{,}074$) traps at $\approx 0.09$ across nearly all $\lambda > 0$ and $D$ values.
    When it escapes (e.g., $\lambda = 1$, $D = 128$), performance matches the plain baseline.
    \item The small model ($N = 4{,}482$) is less susceptible: it matches or nearly matches the baseline at $\lambda \le 1$ for most dataset sizes, but still traps occasionally at larger $D$.
    \item No value of $\lambda$ consistently improves over the plain baseline.
    \item The pattern is non-monotonic in $\lambda$: trapping depends on the interaction between prior strength, model capacity, and random seed --- unlike the midpoint prior's smooth dose--response curve.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Individual seed analysis.}
Examining per-seed results at $\lambda = 0.1$ (large model) reveals the bimodal structure:

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
$D$ & Seed 101 & Seed 202 & Seed 303 & Trap rate \\
\midrule
128  & 0.003 \checkmark & 0.081 $\times$ & 0.003 \checkmark & 1/3 \\

\bottomrule

The trap error ($\approx 0.09$) is a genuine bad local minimum, not a failure to train: an untrained large model gives test error $\approx 0.32$.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
The conservation constraint $H(\hat{\bm{x}}_T) = H(\bm{x}_0)$ restricts predictions to a 1-dimensional level curve of $H$.
While this manifold contains the correct target, the conservation loss gradient is degenerate along the manifold direction (zero component tangent to the level curve).
This creates ridge-like structures in the combined loss landscape.
Larger models, with their higher-dimensional parameter space, appear more susceptible to these spurious attractors.

\subsection{Composite midpoint prior: recovered scaling}
\label{sec:results-composite}

The composite two-step midpoint prior (\cref{sec:composite-prior}) produces scaling behavior qualitatively different from both the single-step midpoint and the conservation prior.
Representative test errors at large capacity ($N = 67{,}074$):

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lrrrr}
\toprule
& $D = 64$ & $D = 512$ & $D = 2048$ & $D = 8192$ \\
\midrule
Plain       & $0.0049 \pm 0.0005$ & $0.0011 \pm 0.0000$ & $0.0009 \pm 0.0000$ & $0.0008 \pm 0.0000$ \\
Midpoint    & $0.0198 \pm 0.0007$ & $0.0159 \pm 0.0001$ & $0.0150 \pm 0.0005$ & $0.0143 \pm 0.0002$ \\
Composite   & $0.0053 \pm 0.0005$ & $0.0018 \pm 0.0001$ & $0.0013 \pm 0.0001$ & $0.0012 \pm 0.0000$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The composite prior is $3$--$18\times$ better than the single-step midpoint across all configurations, but remains $\sim\!1.1$--$1.6\times$ worse than the plain baseline.
The key insight emerges from the scaling fits rather than the absolute error levels:
\[
\hat{E}_\infty \approx 0.001,\quad \hat{\alpha} \approx 2.0\;[0.26,\, 2.76],\quad \hat{\beta} \approx 1.0\;[0.64,\, 1.63].
\]
The composite prior recovers healthy scaling with a near-zero error floor, and the point estimates of its exponents ($\hat{\alpha} \approx 2.0$, $\hat{\beta} \approx 1.0$) are at least as steep as those of the plain baseline ($\hat{\alpha} \approx 0.93$, $\hat{\beta} \approx 0.87$).
The wide bootstrap intervals (especially on $\hat{\alpha}$) caution against overinterpreting the exact values, but the directional pattern is robust: the composite prior remains responsive to both data and capacity, unlike the single-step midpoint prior.

The $\sim\!24\times$ reduction in ground-truth physics-loss residual (from $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{mid}}^* \approx 0.151$ to $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{comp}}^* \approx 0.006$) shifts the model from the approximation-limited regime---where the biased prior dominates---to a regime where both the data and physics gradients provide useful signal.
The remaining $E_\infty \approx 0.001$ floor reflects the residual (but much smaller) discretization bias of the two-step scheme.

\paragraph{Comparison across capacities.}
At medium capacity ($N = 17{,}154$), the composite model achieves $E = 0.0076$ at $D = 64$ and $E = 0.0012$ at $D = 8192$---close to the plain baseline.
At xlarge capacity ($N = 132{,}866$), the composite achieves $E = 0.0049$ at $D = 64$ and $E = 0.0011$ at $D = 8192$, with the gap to plain narrowing as capacity grows.

\subsection{Training stability}

All 1{,}080 scaling-law runs (360 each for plain, midpoint, and composite) completed without divergence or NaN.
The midpoint PIML model early-stops substantially earlier: best-epoch values are typically 50--100 versus 300--400 for the plain model, consistent with the optimizer saturating at the physics-loss plateau.
The composite model trains for durations comparable to the plain model, reflecting the continued utility of both data and physics gradients.

The conservation-law prior introduces a distinct stability concern: not divergence, but bimodal convergence.
Approximately 40--70\% of large-model seeds trap in a bad local minimum at test error $\approx 0.09$, while the remainder converge normally.
The small model shows lower trap rates but is not immune.
This stochastic failure mode is qualitatively different from the midpoint prior's deterministic degradation.

\subsection{Scaling ansatz robustness: alternative functional forms}
\label{sec:results-ansatz}

A potential concern with the scaling results above is sensitivity to the assumed functional form (\cref{eq:scaling}).
To address this, we fit five alternative ansatzes to the full error surface of each model:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, label=\textbf{\Alph*.}, itemsep=0.2em]
    \item $E = E_\infty + a \cdot N^{-\alpha} + b \cdot D^{-\beta}$ \hfill (additive with floor; current default)
    \item $E = a \cdot N^{-\alpha} + b \cdot D^{-\beta}$ \hfill (no floor; $E_\infty \equiv 0$)
    \item $E = c \cdot N^{-\alpha} \cdot D^{-\beta}$ \hfill (multiplicative separable)
    \item $E = E_\infty + a \cdot N^{-\alpha} + b \cdot D^{-\beta} + d \cdot N^{-\alpha} D^{-\beta}$ \hfill (additive + interaction)
    \item Thin-plate spline on $\log N \times \log D \to \log E$ \hfill (nonparametric baseline)
\end{enumerate}

We compare ansatzes using BIC (which penalizes model complexity) and three held-out cross-validation strategies: leave-one-column-out (hold out all data at one $D$), leave-one-row-out (hold out one $N$), and leave-one-corner-out (extrapolation to the largest $(N,D)$ cell).

\paragraph{BIC ranking.}
\Cref{tab:ansatz-bic} summarizes the BIC ranking across models.
Among the parametric ansatzes (A--D), Ansatz~D (interaction) ranks first for all three models, indicating that the additive separable form (Ansatz~A) systematically underestimates joint capacity--data effects.
The nonparametric baseline (E) achieves best overall BIC but uses $\sim\!15$--$20$ effective parameters versus 3--6 for the parametric forms.
For the plain model, the multiplicative Ansatz~C outperforms both additive forms (A, B), consistent with the near-zero $E_\infty$: when there is no error floor, the additive floor parameter is wasted.

\paragraph{Implications for the main results.}
The ansatz comparison confirms two key findings.
First, the qualitative scaling signatures identified in \cref{tab:scaling-params}---floor-raising for midpoint, scaling recovery for composite, near-zero floor for plain---are not artifacts of the assumed functional form.
Ansatz~D captures the same distinctions with improved residuals (RSS $\sim\!2\times$ lower than Ansatz~A for the plain model).
Second, the interaction term $d \cdot N^{-\alpha} D^{-\beta}$ reveals that capacity and data scaling are not fully separable: the fitted interaction coefficient is substantial ($d \approx 0.5$--$13$), particularly for the midpoint and composite models.
This suggests that the physics prior mediates a joint capacity--data effect that the additive ansatz misses.

\section{Ground-Truth Residual Diagnostics}
\label{sec:gt-residuals}

Before training any models on the new systems, we measure the irreducible physics-loss residual for each prior on each system by evaluating the loss on exact ground-truth flow-map targets.
This model-free diagnostic predicts which priors will raise error floors before any experiments are run.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \caption{Ground-truth physics-loss residual $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{phys}}^*$ for each prior on each system at $T = 1.0$.
    Lower values indicate less discretization bias.
    The structural prior column shows the conservation or dissipation loss as appropriate.}
    \label{tab:gt-residuals}
    \small
    \begin{tabular}{lcccc}
        \toprule
        System & Midpoint & Composite & Simpson & Structural \\
        \midrule
        Lotka--Volterra & $0.151$ & $6.2 \times 10^{-3}$ & $4.4 \times 10^{-4}$ & $3.3 \times 10^{-14}$ (conservation) \\

Several patterns are immediately apparent.
First, the midpoint residual varies by more than $20\times$ across systems ($0.15$ to $3.25$), reflecting differences in trajectory curvature.
Second, the bias-reduction series is consistent: composite reduces the midpoint residual by $8$--$24\times$, and Simpson reduces it by a further $3$--$14\times$, across all systems.
Third, the structural prior residual is bimodal in character: on conservative systems (LV, Duffing), it is at machine epsilon ($\sim\!10^{-14}$); on non-conservative Van~der~Pol, the dissipation-rate approximation yields a residual ($3.50$) comparable to the midpoint---predicting that this prior will behave like a biased residual prior rather than an exact structural one.

These residual magnitudes generate specific, testable predictions for the scaling experiments that follow.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{A cross-system taxonomy of prior-induced scaling regimes}

The central finding is that physics priors induce distinct scaling regimes that recur across three dynamical systems with qualitatively different structure (\cref{tab:cross-system}).
We organize these into a diagnostic taxonomy:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Floor-dominated (biased residual).}
    The single-step midpoint prior produces a near-zero data-scaling slope on every system tested ($-0.01$ to $-0.06$).
    The floor magnitude tracks the ground-truth residual: LV ($0.15 \to 1.4\%$ floor), VdP ($3.25 \to 11\%$), Duffing ($1.72 \to 13\%$).
    This is the most robust finding: biased residual priors universally raise error floors, and the floor is quantitatively predictable from a model-free diagnostic (\cref{tab:gt-residuals}).

    \item \textbf{Partial recovery (reduced-bias residual).}
    The Simpson prior ($O(h^5)$ local truncation) recovers intermediate scaling slopes ($-0.17$ to $-0.32$) on all three systems, consistently better than midpoint and consistently worse than plain.
    The composite two-step midpoint on LV shows even steeper recovery ($\hat{\alpha} \approx 2.0$, $\hat{\beta} \approx 1.0$, with wide CIs).
    The degree of recovery is monotonically linked to the GT residual reduction, establishing a dose--response relationship between discretization accuracy and scaling behavior.

    \item \textbf{System-dependent structural.}
    The structural prior (conservation or dissipation) is the only prior whose regime classification changes across systems.
    On conservative Duffing, it produces healthy scaling (slope $-0.44$, near-plain).
    On LV, it is exact but informationally weak, producing bimodal optimization failure that is resolvable via scheduling (\cref{sec:conservation-rescue}).
    On non-conservative VdP, the dissipation surrogate introduces bias comparable to midpoint and produces floor-dominated scaling (slope $-0.06$).
    The explanatory variable is whether the targeted quantity is an exact invariant of the system.
\end{enumerate}

These regime types are \emph{not} visible in pointwise comparisons.
At any single $(N,D)$ configuration, physics-regularized models may underperform the plain baseline in absolute terms; only the scaling surface reveals whether the prior destroys, partially recovers, or preserves healthy scaling behavior.

\subsection{Why the midpoint floor is universal}

The floor-raising mechanism is the same across all three systems: the midpoint-rule residual approximates $\int_0^T F(\bm{x}(t))\,dt$ with a single evaluation at $(\bm{x}_0 + \bm{x}_T)/2$.
As the data loss decreases during training, the biased physics gradient grows relative to the data gradient:
\[
\frac{\lambda \cdot \nabla \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{mid}}}{\nabla \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{data}}} \sim \frac{\lambda \cdot O(1)}{O(\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{data}})},
\]
eventually dominating the optimization and producing a plateau.
The floor height scales with the GT residual magnitude, which depends on the trajectory curvature of each system at the chosen horizon.
This mechanism explains why the floor is highest on Duffing (strongest curvature from cubic nonlinearity) and lowest on LV.

\subsection{Why bias reduction recovers scaling}

The composite and Simpson priors reduce the GT residual by $8$--$24\times$ and $14$--$300\times$ respectively relative to midpoint, delaying or eliminating the gradient-ratio crossover point.
The recovered scaling slopes track the residual reduction monotonically across all systems, establishing a causal link.
On LV, the composite prior achieves near-zero error floor ($\hat{E}_\infty \approx 0.001$) with exponents at least as steep as the plain baseline (noting wide bootstrap intervals: $\hat{\alpha} \in [0.26, 2.76]$).
The practical implication is that for ODE-residual priors, the \emph{accuracy of the numerical discretization} determines whether the prior helps or hurts in the scaling limit---a diagnosis available before any training runs.

\subsection{Why structural priors are system-dependent}

The conservation constraint $H(\hat{\bm{x}}_T) = H(\bm{x}_0)$ restricts predictions to a 1-dimensional level curve containing the correct target.
Whether this helps depends on two conditions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Exactness}: the targeted quantity must be exactly conserved.
    On VdP, the dissipation-rate balance is approximate (GT residual $3.50$), and the prior behaves like a biased residual---falling into the floor-dominated regime.
    \item \textbf{Informational sufficiency}: the constraint must resolve the output.
    On Duffing, the energy $E_D = \frac{1}{2}y^2 + \frac{1}{2}\alpha_D x^2 + \frac{1}{4}\beta_D x^4$ constrains a 2D output sufficiently (slope $-0.44$, no bimodality).
    On LV, $H$ is exact but constrains only one scalar on a 2D output; the conservation gradient vanishes along the level curve tangent, creating degenerate directions that produce bimodal failure at high capacity.
    The rescue analysis confirms this is an optimizer artifact: lambda-ramp scheduling eliminates all bimodality (0/162 configs), though the informational floor at $\sim\!8\%$ persists.
\end{enumerate}

This result extends the single-system observation from the anchor study into a mechanistic principle: structural priors are not universally helpful or harmful---their scaling behavior is determined by the match between the constraint and the system's geometry.

\subsection{Practical implications}

The cross-system results refine the practical workflow for evaluating physics priors:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Compute the GT residual} on exact or high-quality reference targets.
    A nonzero residual predicts the floor height; the ratio between GT residuals for two priors predicts their relative scaling behavior.
    This diagnostic generalizes across systems (\cref{tab:gt-residuals}).
    \item \textbf{Check structural-prior exactness} before deploying conservation or balance-law penalties.
    An approximate structural prior (e.g., dissipation on VdP) behaves like a biased residual prior, not like an exact constraint.
    \item \textbf{Run a $\lambda$-sweep} to distinguish prior classes.
    Monotone degradation indicates biased-residual mismatch; non-monotone, seed-sensitive behavior indicates optimization-geometry failure.
    \item \textbf{Inspect the joint $(N,D)$ surface} rather than benchmark scores.
    Distinct priors producing similar pointwise underperformance may occupy fundamentally different scaling regimes.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Connection to scaling-law theory}

Bahri et al.~\cite{bahri2024} explain neural scaling laws through variance-limited and resolution-limited regimes.
Our results identify two additional regimes specific to SciML:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item An \emph{approximation-limited} regime where the irreducible bias of the physics loss creates a floor that dominates both variance and resolution components.
    This regime manifests for midpoint priors on all three systems and for the dissipation prior on VdP.
    \item An \emph{optimization-limited} regime where loss landscape geometry determines performance.
    This manifests for the conservation prior on LV but not on Duffing, indicating that the regime depends on the prior--system interaction.
\end{itemize}

The cross-system evidence strengthens the case that these additional regimes are not artifacts of a single system.
The approximation-limited regime recurs universally, while the optimization-limited regime recurs only when geometric conditions (informational insufficiency of the constraint) are met.
Extending scaling-law theory to accommodate structured loss functions and constrained architectures remains an open theoretical challenge.

\section{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=0.3em]
    \item The study covers three 2D autonomous ODE systems.
    All are low-dimensional, non-chaotic, and deterministic; conclusions should not be overgeneralized to PDE learning, chaotic systems, high-dimensional systems, or real-world scientific datasets with measurement noise.
    \item The cross-system results (VdP, Duffing) use a pilot grid of 2 seeds $\times$ 3 capacities $\times$ 4 dataset sizes, smaller than the full LV sweep.
    Pilot scaling slopes are indicative but carry wider uncertainty than the anchor results.
    \item It compares a plain MLP against four soft-constraint regularizers; hard architectural constraints, equivariant networks, and operator-learning approaches are not tested.
    \item The task is fixed-horizon flow-map prediction at $T = 1.0$, not long-horizon rollout, and all data are synthetic and noise-free.
    \item The scaling fits are descriptive.
    Although the ansatz comparison (\cref{sec:results-ansatz}) shows qualitative robustness on LV, the fitted exponents should not be treated as universal constants.
    The composite prior's estimated capacity exponent has wide uncertainty ($\hat{\alpha} \in [0.26, 2.76]$), so the main claim should be read as ``healthy low-floor scaling is recovered,'' not as a precise quantitative law.
    \item No prior tested here improves over the plain baseline in absolute test error; the composite prior's advantage is visible only in scaling exponents and error floor.
    \item The conservation-law bimodality on LV depends on the chosen optimizer and can be reduced by lambda-ramp scheduling (\cref{sec:conservation-rescue}), but the informational floor persists.
    \item The ansatz comparison reveals a non-negligible interaction term ($d \cdot N^{-\alpha}D^{-\beta}$), suggesting the additive separable ansatz used for the main results is a simplification.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}

We introduced empirical scaling laws as a diagnostic framework for evaluating physics priors in scientific machine learning and tested it across three dynamical systems---Lotka--Volterra, Van der Pol, and Duffing---spanning conservative, dissipative, and conservative-with-cubic-nonlinearity dynamics.
Across more than 2{,}300 runs (including 810 rescue configurations), four prior types produced four qualitatively distinct scaling signatures, three of which recur across all systems tested.

A biased single-step ODE-residual prior raises the error floor and compresses scaling exponents universally; the floor height is quantitatively predicted by the model-free ground-truth residual diagnostic.
A reduced-bias Simpson prior partially recovers scaling on all three systems, establishing a dose--response link between discretization accuracy and scaling behavior.
A composite two-step midpoint prior achieves near-full recovery on LV, the system where it was tested in depth.
An exact structural prior (conservation or dissipation) is the only prior whose regime classification is system-dependent: it produces healthy scaling on Duffing where the invariant is informationally sufficient, bimodal failure on LV where it is informationally weak (resolvable via scheduling), and floor-dominated scaling on VdP where the targeted quantity is approximate.

The main contribution is methodological.
Scaling behavior itself can serve as an evaluation object in scientific machine learning.
Priors should be judged not only by whether they improve one benchmark score, but by how they reshape the joint dependence of error on data, model size, and optimization.
The cross-system evidence shows that universal regimes (floor-dominated, partial recovery) coexist with system-dependent regimes (structural-prior behavior), and that both are diagnosable before committing to large-scale experiments.
That makes scaling-law analysis a promising tool for more principled evaluation of physical inductive biases in future scientific learning studies.

\section{Conservation-Law Prior: Details}
\label{app:conservation-details}

The Lotka--Volterra first integral is
\[
H(u,v) = \delta u - \gamma \ln u + \beta v - \alpha \ln v.
\]
The conservation loss penalizes $(\Delta H)^2 = (H(\hat{\bm{x}}_T) - H(\bm{x}_0))^2$.
On ground-truth data, $|\Delta H| / |H| \approx 10^{-7}$, confirming the prior is exact to within numerical integration tolerances.

The conservation loss operates in physical (denormalized) space.
Float32 normalization roundtrip introduces $\sim 10^{-7}$ maximum error, which is negligible.
All training data satisfies strict positivity ($u > 0$, $v > 0$), ensuring the logarithmic terms in $H$ are well-defined.
No negative predictions were observed from untrained models within the tested initial-condition domain $[0.5, 2.5]^2$.

The bimodal optimization failure described in \cref{sec:results-conservation} is not caused by numerical issues in the loss computation, but by the geometric structure of the conservation constraint: $H(u,v) = c$ defines a closed curve in $(u,v)$ space, and the conservation gradient is zero along this curve.
This creates a degenerate direction in the combined loss landscape that becomes problematic as model capacity increases.
